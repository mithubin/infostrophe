Was wurde finanziert?

Carl Bernstein (TIME Magazine, 24. Februar 1992):

"Tons of equipment - fax machines (the first in Poland), printing presses, transmitters, telephones, shortwave radios, video cameras, photocopiers, telex machines, computers, word processors - were smuggled into Poland via channels established by priests and American agents and representatives of the AFL-CIO and European labor movements. Money for the banned union came from CIA funds, the National Endowment for Democracy, secret accounts in the Vatican and Western trade unions."

Das Brüsseler Büro als Drehscheibe:

"The Solidarity office in Brussels became an international clearinghouse: for representatives of the Vatican, for CIA operatives, for the AFL-CIO, for representatives of the Socialist International, for the congressionally funded National Endowment for Democracy... It was the place where Solidarity told its backers — some of whose real identities were unknown to Solidarity itself — what it needed."

Die Rolle von Radio Free Europe (RFE)

RFE wurde bis 1971 direkt von der CIA finanziert, danach offiziell vom US-Kongress. Aber:

US State Department (Historical Documents):

  • Sowjetunion beschuldigte RFE, "antisozialistische" Aktivitäten in Polen zu orchestrieren
  • Prawda-Kommentator Yuriy Zhukov: RFE "guides" die unabhängige Solidarność
  • Sowjets behaupteten, RFE sende "codierte" oder "geheime" Nachrichten an konspirative Elemente in Polen

Polish Intelligence Report (Mai 1983, Wilson Center): RFE-Chef Zdzislaw Najder erhielt CIA-Instruktionen:

  • "Intensify RFE initiatives aimed at increasing socio-political tensions in Poland during martial law"
  • "Create within the shortest possible time... a central base abroad, which would coordinate the activity of the 'underground movement' in Poland"
  • "Assist with the creation of a financial base abroad for the building of an 'underground state' in Poland"
  • "Expand the network of RFE collaborators inside Poland as a covert group which... would in fact carry out espionage for the CIA"

James F. Brown (RFE-Direktor 1978-1984): spielte eine "seminal behind-the-scenes role in the rise of the Solidarity movement" (Boston Globe).

Die Gründung des National Endowment for Democracy (NED)

Das neue Gesicht der Covert Action

LAWCHA (Labor and Working-Class History Association):

"An odd assortment of US neoconservatives and anticommunist social democrats... came to believe that it would be far more efficient if Washington could bankroll Solidarność out in the open, and free from any association with the CIA and its bad reputation. To this end, in 1983, the Reagan administration and AFL-CIO teamed up to convince Congress to establish a new, quasi-private foundation to provide US government funds to anticommunist civil society organizations abroad, including unions."

NED-Gründer Allen Weinstein (Washington Post, 1991):

"A lot of what we do today was done covertly 25 years ago by the CIA."

New York Times (1986):

"The NED resembles the aid given by the Central Intelligence Agency in the 1950's, 60's and 70's to bolster pro-American political groups, with the main difference being that the NED operated out in the open."

NED-Präsident Carl Gershman:

"It would be terrible for democratic groups around the world to be seen as subsidized by the CIA... We saw that in the 60's, and that's why it has been discontinued. We have not had the capability of doing this, and that's why the endowment was created."

Ironischerweise: AFL-CIO-Officials lobbten im Kongress für NED-Gründung – mit stärkster Unterstützung von anti-gewerkschaftlichen Republikanern wie Senator Orrin Hatch (hatte Jahre zuvor Gewerkschaftsrechte für US-Arbeiter blockiert).
